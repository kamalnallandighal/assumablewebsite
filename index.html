Frequently Asked Questions

Everything you need to know about assumable mortgages

  • An assumable mortgage allows a homebuyer to take over the seller's existing home loan — including its interest rate, repayment period, and remaining balance. This is especially valuable when the seller locked in a low rate years ago, which the new buyer inherits rather than taking out a new loan at today's higher rates.
  • To assume a mortgage, you typically need to meet the lender's credit and income requirements — usually a credit score of 620+ and a debt-to-income ratio under 43%. The process is similar to qualifying for a new mortgage, but you benefit from the seller's locked-in rate. Jeff can walk you through a free pre-screening to confirm your eligibility.
  • All properties on this platform are pre-screened and clearly marked as assumable. Use the "Assumable Only" filter on our property search page to browse exclusively homes where you can lock in a below-market rate. Each listing shows the current loan rate alongside the market rate so you can see your savings at a glance.
  • With today's rates above 6%, assuming a 2–3% loan can save you $500–$1,000+ per month on a typical Arizona home — that's $6,000–$12,000 per year. Over the life of a 30-year loan, the savings can exceed $200,000. You also benefit from lower closing costs since no new appraisal or origination fees are required.
  • Yes — in an assumption, your down payment covers the seller's equity (the difference between the home's price and the remaining loan balance). You'll need at least 5% in cash, and the gap can often be covered with secondary financing. Many assumable listings on our platform have low equity requirements, making them very accessible.
  • Government-backed loans — VA and FHA — are assumable by law. Conventional loans (Fannie Mae/Freddie Mac) are generally not assumable. On our platform, all flagged listings are VA or FHA loans, meaning the assumption right is guaranteed. The loan type is shown on every listing card.
